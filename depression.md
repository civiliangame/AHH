# Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

A major depressive episode (Criteria A–C below) is the diagnostic building block of MDD.

**Criterion A — ≥5 of 9 symptoms during the same 2-week period**, representing a change from prior functioning. At least one symptom must be (1) depressed mood or (2) loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia). Symptoms (each present nearly every day unless otherwise noted):
1. Depressed mood most of the day (subjective report or observed; in children/adolescents may present as irritable mood).
2. Markedly diminished interest or pleasure in all or almost all activities most of the day.
3. Significant unintentional weight change (e.g., >5% of body weight in a month) or appetite change.
4. Insomnia or hypersomnia.
5. Psychomotor agitation or retardation (observable by others, not merely subjective).
6. Fatigue or loss of energy.
7. Feelings of worthlessness or excessive/inappropriate guilt (may be delusional; not merely self-reproach about being ill).
8. Diminished ability to think or concentrate, or indecisiveness.
9. Recurrent thoughts of death, recurrent suicidal ideation without a specific plan, or a suicide attempt or specific plan.

**Criterion B — Functional impairment:** the symptoms cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

**Criterion C — Substance/medical exclusion:** the episode is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition.

Criteria A–C constitute a major depressive episode. For the disorder diagnosis, two further criteria apply:
- **Criterion D:** the episode is not better explained by schizoaffective disorder, schizophrenia, schizophreniform disorder, delusional disorder, or other specified/unspecified schizophrenia spectrum and psychotic disorders.
- **Criterion E:** there has never been a manic or hypomanic episode (unless these were substance-induced or attributable to a medical condition). Per StatPearls, ruling out a history of mania/hypomania is essential to distinguish MDD from bipolar disorder.

**Bereavement note (DSM-5/DSM-5-TR change):** DSM-IV excluded an MDD diagnosis for depressive symptoms lasting <2 months following the death of a loved one (the "bereavement exclusion"). This was removed in DSM-5. A clinical note now directs clinicians to use judgment to distinguish a normal grief response to a significant loss (bereavement, financial ruin, natural disaster, serious medical illness/disability) from a major depressive episode. The APA's stated rationale: bereavement is recognized to commonly last longer than the 2-month period implied by DSM-IV; bereavement is a severe psychosocial stressor that can precipitate a true MDE in vulnerable individuals; and bereavement-related depression responds to treatment like depression in other contexts. Clinically useful differentiators noted by the APA: in grief, self-esteem is usually preserved and dysphoria comes in waves tied to thoughts of the deceased, whereas in MDE pervasive depressed mood and corrosive feelings of worthlessness and self-loathing predominate.

**Specifiers:** with anxious distress, mixed features, melancholic features, atypical features, psychotic features, catatonia, peripartum onset, seasonal pattern; plus severity (mild/moderate/severe) and remission status.
