# Bipolar II Disorder

Bipolar II requires **at least one hypomanic episode AND at least one major depressive episode**, with the lifetime absence of any manic episode.

**Hypomanic Episode:**

**Criterion A:** A distinct period of abnormally and persistently elevated, expansive, or irritable mood AND abnormally and persistently increased activity or energy, lasting at least 4 consecutive days, present most of the day, nearly every day.

**Criterion B:** ≥3 symptoms (≥4 if mood only irritable) from the same 7-symptom list used for mania, persisting, representing a noticeable change from usual behavior, and present to a significant degree.

**Criterion C:** The episode is associated with an unequivocal change in functioning that is uncharacteristic of the individual when not symptomatic.

**Criterion D:** The disturbance in mood and the change in functioning are observable by others.

**Criterion E:** The episode is **NOT** severe enough to cause marked impairment in social or occupational functioning or to necessitate hospitalization. **If there are psychotic features, the episode is, by definition, manic** (not hypomanic).

**Criterion F:** The episode is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance. (The same antidepressant-emergent note applies: a full hypomanic episode persisting at a fully syndromal level beyond the physiological effect of treatment counts; however, the DSM cautions that one or two symptoms—particularly increased irritability, edginess, or agitation following antidepressant use—should not alone be taken as evidence of a hypomanic episode or a bipolar diathesis.)

**Diagnostic criteria for the disorder:**
- **Criterion A:** Criteria have been met for at least one hypomanic episode and at least one major depressive episode.
- **Criterion B:** There has never been a manic episode.
- **Criterion C (DSM-5-TR wording):** at least one hypomanic episode and at least one major depressive episode are not better explained by schizoaffective disorder and are not superimposed on schizophrenia, schizophreniform disorder, delusional disorder, or other specified/unspecified schizophrenia spectrum and psychotic disorders.
- **Criterion D:** The symptoms of depression OR the unpredictability caused by frequent alternation between periods of depression and hypomania cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

A key clinical pitfall: Bipolar II is frequently misdiagnosed as unipolar MDD because depressive episodes dominate the clinical picture, depressive episodes tend to be longer and more frequent than in Bipolar I, and patients rarely present during—or volunteer a history of—hypomania (which they may experience as desirable productivity).

## Recommendations
- **Always screen for a lifetime history of mania/hypomania before diagnosing MDD.** Conversion from unipolar depression to bipolar disorder is meaningful and time-dependent: Kessing et al. (2017, *Bipolar Disorders*) found a 12.9% conversion rate over 10 years, with the yearly conversion rate falling from ~3.9% in the first year to ~0.8% in years 5–10; Ratheesh et al. (2017, *Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica*) found 22.5% over 12–18 years. A positive history reclassifies the diagnosis and changes pharmacotherapy.
- **Use a collateral informant for suspected hypomania.** Hypomanic Criteria C–D require that the change in functioning be unequivocal and observable by others; relying on patient self-report alone systematically underdetects Bipolar II.
- **For grief presentations, apply clinical judgment per the DSM-5 bereavement note** rather than an automatic 2-month exclusion; diagnose and treat a superimposed MDE when present (e.g., marked worthlessness, suicidality, psychomotor retardation, or functional incapacity beyond expected grief).
- **Threshold that changes management:** any episode meeting full manic criteria—marked impairment, hospitalization, or psychotic features—shifts the diagnosis from Bipolar II to Bipolar I and alters treatment (mood stabilization rather than, or before, antidepressant use).

## Caveats
- These are paraphrased summaries for clinical reference; consult DSM-5-TR directly for verbatim criteria, full specifier definitions, and coding.
- Prevalence figures vary by survey and reporting convention—e.g., NIMH/NCS-R (2.7% 12-month, 5.7% lifetime) versus ADAA (3.1%, ~6.8 million adults)—reflecting methodological and rounding differences, not contradictions.
- StatPearls' abbreviated GAD criteria list is imprecise (it duplicates "irritability" and does not cleanly label criteria A–F); the APA DSM-5-TR text is the authoritative source.
- Episode-duration thresholds (7 days for mania, 4 days for hypomania) remain debated—some experts (e.g., per *Psychiatric Times* coverage of an AREDOC task force) advocate shorter durations—but DSM-5-TR retains the standard thresholds.
